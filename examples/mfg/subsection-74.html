Subsection 2.3 Finding a Formula for Direct Variation

If we know any one pair of values for the variables in a direct variation, we can find the constant of variation. We can then use the constant to write a formula for one of the variables as a function of the other.

Example 2.3.6.

If an object is dropped from a great height, its speed, \(v\text{,}\) varies directly with the time, \(t\text{,}\) the object has been falling. A rock dropped off the rim of the Grand Canyon is falling at a speed of \(39.2\) meters per second when it passes a lizard on a ledge \(4\) seconds later.

  1. Express \(v\) as a function of \(t\text{.}\)
  2. What is the speed of the rock after it has fallen for 6 seconds?
  3. Sketch a graph of \(v(t)\) versus \(t\text{.}\)
Solution

  1. Because \(v\) varies directly with \(t\text{,}\) there is a positive constant \(k\) for which \(v = kt\text{.}\) We substitute \(v = 39.2\) when \(t = 4\) and solve for \(k\) to find

    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned}[t] 39.2 \amp = k(4)\amp\amp \blert{\text{Divide both sides by 4.}}\\ k \amp = 9.8 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    Thus, \(v (t) = 9.8 t\text{.}\)

  2. We evaluate the function found in part (a) for \(t = 6\text{.}\)

    \begin{equation*} v(\alert{6}) = 9.8(\alert{6}) = 58.8 \end{equation*}

    At \(t = 6\) seconds, the rock is falling at a speed of \(58.8\) meters per second.

Checkpoint 2.3.7.

The volume of a bag of rice, in cups, is directly proportional to the weight of the bag. A \(2\)-pound bag contains \(3.5\) cups of rice.

  1. Express the volume, \(V\text{,}\) of a bag of rice as a function of its weight, \(w\text{.}\)
  2. How many cups of rice are in a \(15\)-pound bag?
Answer

  1. \(V = 1.75w\)

  2. \(26.25\)
